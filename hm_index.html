What's Elite About This Plan
✓
Norwegian 4×4 is the right call
Your Garmin splits from that long run show you holding 5:40–5:55/km for 16.2km — solid aerobic base. The 4×4 protocol will directly target the 5:07/km ceiling. This is the most evidence-based HRmax interval protocol in endurance sport right now.
✓
The fatigue-stacking on stations is correct
Ottawa showed RPE 10 on wall balls and lunges while running easy. The plan correctly diagnoses this as a fatigue-specificity problem, not a strength problem. Training stations post-interval is exactly how to fix it.
✓
Your current trajectory is real
Garmin's prediction shows 1:49:20 HM improving by 5 min in 30 days. That's genuine aerobic adaptation showing up. If that rate continues even at half speed, you hit 1:45 comfortably.
What I'd Challenge
⚠
Double sessions on Tue/Thu are aggressive for a 9–5
6am intervals + evening stations on back-to-back days is a 48hr fatigue window that most club athletes don't recover from properly. Your long run pace data from the last screenshot shows HR 143–180 within 5km — that's an enormous range suggesting inconsistent recovery. I'd trial Tue stations 4hrs minimum after the intervals, not same evening.
⚠
53km peak week with gym ×2 needs honest sleep accounting
Week 11 is 53km + 2 gym sessions + station work. At 8hrs sleep that's manageable. At 6hrs with work stress it's an overreaching risk. Build a hard rule now: if sleep <7hrs two nights in a row, drop Tuesday to easy and push the interval to Thursday. Non-negotiable.
⚠
Your recent 5km interval session HR (143–180) needs attention
The pace analysis screenshot shows 5 intervals with HR going 143 → 164 → 172 → 179 → 180 across 5km. That's a strong cardiac drift — either you started too fast, your aerobic base isn't yet there for the target pace, or you weren't recovered. The 4:51–5:09 paces are already close to HM target. Good sign — but you're riding close to the edge early. Monitor this closely in weeks 5–8.
What I'd Add
+
Add a weekly HRV or perceived readiness check
Every Sunday morning, rate sleep quality (1–5) + perceived readiness (1–5). If combined score ≤6, reduce Tuesday's intensity by one notch. This costs you nothing and prevents 80% of overtraining injuries in double-session blocks.
+
Add a wall ball breathing drill to every warm-up
30 reps with a focus only on exhale timing — no weight, just a medicine ball. Do this before Tuesday and Wednesday stations regardless of the session. This builds the motor pattern so it's automatic under race fatigue. 4 minutes. Ottawa was lost in the breathing.
+
Set a long run HR ceiling, not pace target
Your Saturday splits show 5:28–5:56/km with HR 151–156 mid-run. That's the right zone. But add a rule: if HR hits 158 before km 12, slow down. Long runs done too hard are the most common reason runners miss their taper feeling good. Protect easy as easy.
Bottom Line

This is a well-constructed plan for someone training dual-goal with a real work schedule. The diagnosis of Ottawa is accurate. The prescription is correct. The risk is execution discipline — not the plan itself. The athletes who hit sub-1:25 HYROX with a sub-1:45 HM two weeks later aren't doing more training than this. They're doing this training better: showing up recovered, keeping easy days easy, and being ruthless about the hard sessions.
